    Technical writing

    Technical writing is a very good skill to have, and many have talked about why.

    Lots of people think writing requires inspiration, a muse, or special talent.

    While these may (or not) be necessary for writing a fiction masterpiece, they certainly aren't needed to write pretty good technical stuff.

    In reality it doesn't need any of that. it's a technical skill that can be learned and perfected with practice. and there are methods.

    the reason I've seen most people struggle with technical writing is because they lack a process that helps them remove blockers. A common blocker is getting out of ideas. Another common blocker is you know what you want to say, but you don't know how to say it nicely. So you keep playing with it in your head and never write it down. Another blocker is that you're stuck between sections that feel unconected, or you cannot continue after one idea that's already polished.

    the secret to good technical writing is this: you need to edit a lot. Instead of trying to come up with good ideas, you just dump everything down, but you relentlessly edit it, rewrite it, improve it.

    however editing the things we write is hard, and the reason can be reduced to the issue that we get attached to what we spend a lot of time working on, and it becomes hard to undo it, or delete it completely. so the longer we spend trying to come up with a cool way of saying something, the harder it will be to edit it. And no matter how long we spend on it, the first few iterations will be complete crap.

    the key theme in this process is to delay making decisions that will get you attached to your writing to as late as possible. to be in a position where you can shamelessly cut half of what you wrote and don't feel bad about it.

    these is what works for me. it's not a fool-proof process, and maybe they'll work for, maybe they won't. But it's designed to deal with the common blockers one at a time, lack of ideas, lack of originality, ...

    I try to loosely follow these stages:

    • üß† Brain-dump the key ideas
    • üîÉ Make an outline
    • üíß Write the content
    • üçÄ Rewrite for clarity
    • ü§© Rewrite for impact

    üß† Brain-dump the key ideas

    The purpose of this step is to pinpoint the key ideas that you want to get across.

    I just write everything down, not thinking about structure or story. Structure will come later, but for now, these are just keypoints.

    I like to write short sentences, one per line. Sometimes two or three connected sentences. Not more.

    Ideas should be slim, they have no context, they should not need anything around them for you to understand what you meant with it. they should make sense in isolation.

    üîÉ Make an outline

    At this point I have a soup of ideas, some good, some bad, some ugly. It's time to give them some sort of structure.

    The task here is to reorder the ideas so that the similar topics start to cluster together and a structure starts to emerge.

    Sometimes I need to add a short idea here or there to connect something, or rewrite it slightly to make it more meaningful.

    I also add sections, subsections, whatever is necessary for structure.

    But don't try to explain the ideas, don't make them fat yet. Keep them slim. When ideas become paragraphs, they grow roots, they become heavy and hard to reorder. They attach to their context.

    In time, a story starts to emerge, and everything begins to look as part of bigger structure. Play around with different orders, just by moving ideas around. That's why we want them to be slim.

    You may start seeing an overarching theme struggling to bubble through the cracks. Maybe it's meaningful analogy that supports the entire story, maybe it's a tone, or a style thing. Resist the urge to help it, but don't destroy it either.

    When you finish this step, you should have some kind of skeleton, think of it like a blueprint for a garden, you should be able to see the guides as thin cords connecting here and there, the subtle shapes that are starting to take form. Maybe there will be a fountain over there, a bush over here. If you squint your eye, you should be able to imagine your story unfolding over this structure, growing thicker at some points, slim at others. You should see the leaves growing on the guides, so to speak.

    üíß Fill-in the content

    At this point, the purpose is to expand every single idea into a paragraph, or two, or three, as many as necessary.

    Ideally, if your ideas are more or less on the same level of abstraction, they will expand to a similar depth. But you might desire to explore in further depth a specific topic.

    The important thing here is to commit to the structure you created. Why? Because if you deviate too much from it, you'll loose that sense of structure, of story arc, and you'll be at the beginning again, but this time with heavy paragraphs instead of slim ideas.

    And ideas are easy to reorder, but paragraphs grow roots, the connect to other paragraphs around them, and they are almost impossible to move around once they starting taking shape.

    Keep working on the content, adding as much as you need to fully express yourself, don't be terse, don't try to be concise. Enjoy this moment and write down everything you feel you need. Don't worry about grammar, don't worry about dumb or boring stuff. Just write.

    Don't spend too much time thinking any sentence. Why? Because you'll grow attached to them, and it will be hard to cut them down. Don't grow attached to them, just let them grow naturally as they need to.

    Stop when you think that everything that needs to be said has been said. Don't be afraid to repeat yourself.

    üçÄ Rewrite for clarity

    Once all the content is in place, you should have a pretty dense garden, shapes are almost unrecognizable, touching eachother. Now it's time to prune.

    The purpose of this step is to rewrite every paragraph down to a very concise and clear form. You should take every single paragraph and try to say it again, but with something like half the words. Remove everything that seems removable.

    By definition, think any paragraph is useless and just cut it and read again. If you don't miss it, it's gone for good. If you miss it, then Ctrl+Z and go over the sentences. Cut each one out and re-read. Same story. Just doing this you'll discover around half of what you wrote can be cut clean entirely.

    For the remaining half, they are now ugly, kind of shapeless, with holes here and there. You need to refine them.

    Remove every word that seems slightly unecessary. For the rest, try to reword them to use simpler synonims. Reorder the words in a sentence to put the action first. Simplify tenses. Remove pasive voice. Reorder the sentences in a paragraph so that the first sentence summarizes the key idea, and the remaining sentences support it.

    The purpose is to weed out all unnecessary stuff and end with a thiner and leaner version of everything important that you said before, just prettier, and clearer.

    ü§© Rewrite for impact

    Now we have a pretty lean story, nothing is unnecessary, but it's probably kind of gray, dull, using words like "like" and "kind of". It's time to make it blossom.

    Identify the parts that should be meaningful, and decide how you want your reader to feel when reading them. Rewrite sentences to make them bolder, more ambicious. Say things the most impactful way you can, that you still feel confident they're true. Drop qualifiers, instead of "very important" say "crucial". Make every sentence crisper, the kind of sentence that feels like a whiplash when you speak it out loud.

    Remember that hint of an overarching theme that was starting to bubble through the structure? Well, if its really there, its time to get it at the front. Find that theme, that main idea that resonates through out the story and polish it, make it clearer. Repeat it.

    ‚ùì Asking for feedback

    While asking for a general impression might be useful, you will often get much more valuable advice if you are intentional and specific when asking for it.

    ‚ùî In each step, ask for specific advice tailored to the purpose of that step:

    • üëâ which topics or ideas do you think are missing / unnecesary?
    • üëâ which parts did you understand the best / worst?
    • üëâ which parts did you feel the most boring / exciting?

    Ask people to give you specific advice. Something like "highlight the three paragraphs you found the hardest to understand".

    dont try to please everyone. take whatever advice feels good and shamelessly dismiss whatever doesn't fit.

    the method is there to help, but it shouldn't be a force field. if something feels right at some point, do it.

    these steps are just a way to help you get on track when you don't know what to do next.

    i've found is sometimes useful to let the my brain rest between steps, and even to work on completely different drafts. but sometimes I'm on the flow and just do all steps one after the other. it also depends on the length and complexity.

    outro: technical writing is a skill that anyone can learn. it's a hugely valuable skill, and it's easy to get started. just open a blog somewhere.
